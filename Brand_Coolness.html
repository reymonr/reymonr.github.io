Brand Coolness

  • ABSTRACT
    keywords:
attitudes, authenticity, brands, coolness, niche, scale development, structural equation modeling
  • INTRODUCTION
    • consumers spend an enormous amount of money on cool brands.
    • being cool has helped startup brands soar past established competitors
    • being uncool can sink even popular and well-funded brands
    • what makes a brand cool? the answer is unclear
    • all literatures conducted before has not systematically identified the characteristics differentiating cool from uncool brands, nor has it identified how these characteristics change as brands move from being cool within a small subculture (niche cool) to the broader population (mass cool)
    • through a series of studies, this research has generate and validate a measure of brand coolness that incorporates ten characteristics that distinguish cool brands from uncool brands
    • in addition, this research also explore the nomological network related to brand coolness by identifying a set of variables that are related to, yet conceptually distinct from, coolness
    • moreover, this research also examine the subjective and dynamic nature of brand coolness
  • CONCEPTUALIZING COOL
    • cool has many synonyms (hip, awesome, sweet, chill, badass, dope, etc) but is difficult to define
    • over 70 different ways (Web Appendix A) that coolness has been described and defined, illustrates how the literature has not converged on a definition, and highlights the need to establish a firmer, empirically grounded understanding of brand coolness
    • in this study, they use the definition by Warren and Campbell's definition of coolness:
    • four essential features of coolness: subjective, positive valence, autonomy, and dynamic
  • UNANSWERED QUESTIONS
    1. What characteristics are prototypical of cool brands?
    1. Can we develop a validated instrument to measure the component characteristics of cool brands?
    1. What are the consequences of brand coolness?
    1. How do the characteristics and consequences of coolness change as brands move from niche cool to mass cool?
  • IDENTIFYING CHARACTERISTICS: QUALITATIVE RESEARCH
    • three qualitative studies conducted using: focus groups, depth interviews, and essays with consumers from North America and Europe
    • the characteristics were identified by looking for similar patterns of responses across the different methods and cultures utilizing the ATLAS.ti software

    METHOD

    • Focus Groups
      • four focus groups conducted in Western (United Kingdom), Eastern (Slovakia), and Southern (Portugal) Europe
      • eight participants in each group
      • 60 minutes
    • Depth Interviews
      • 30 depth interviews were conducted with consumers in Portugal
      • the interviews followed a methodological procedure similar to McCracken [1988; see also Gubrium and Holstein (2001)]
      • informants (respondents) were asked a series of grand tour questions
    • Essays
      • 75 students at a university in the United States
      • wrote two essays
        • describing a brand they thought was cool
        • describing a brand they liked but did not think was cool

    THEMES IN THE QUALITATIVE DATA

    • Ten themes, or characteristics, related to brand coolness emerged from the focus group, interview, and essay responses
      1. useful/extraordinary
      1. aesthetically appealing
      1. energetic
      1. high status
      1. original
      1. authentic
      1. rebellious
      1. subcultural
      1. iconic
      1. popular

    DIFFERENCES BETWEEN COOL AND UNCOOL BRANDS

    • iconic (73% vs 8%) - symbolize an important value, belief, or memory (only 8% of uncool brands were described as iconic)
    • extraordinary or useful (76% vs 71%) - this did not distinguish cool from uncool brands, as respondents also considered most uncool brands useful (71%)
    • subcultural (44% vs 7%)
    • original (33% vs 4%)
    • aesthetically appealing (25% vs 4%)
    • popular (17% vs 4%)
    • high status (15% vs 4%)
    • energetic (8% vs 0%)

    THEMES ABSENT IN THE QUALITATIVE DATA

    • cultural knowledge
    • emotional concealment, friendliness, and competence

  • STRUCTURAL AND NOMOLOGICAL MODELING: QUANTITATIVE RESEARCH
    • eight survey studies were conducted to identify the higher-order structure of the characteristics of brand coolness that emerged in the qualitative research and to test their nomological relationship with related constructs
    • each study asked respondents to evaluate a brand that they consider cool, a brand that they do not consider cool, or both
    • the first 4 studies were pretests, in which they developed and refined the measures for the structural and nomological models
    • study 5 purposes:
      • confirmed the structural measurement model for the ten characteristics associated with brand coolness
      • confirmed that all ten characteristics were more closely associated with cool brands than uncool brands
      • tested the nomological relationship between brand coolness and related constructs, including brand personality, SBC, brand love, brand attitude, WTP fro the brand, and intentions to spread WOM about the brand
    • study 6 improved the measure of brand coolness by developing items that better capture the extent to which brand seems extraordinary rather than merely useful
    • study 7 used a purely confirmatory design to replicate the results of study 6
    • study 8 examined the dynamic and subjective nature of brand coolness by testing how the characteristics and consequences differ between niche cool brands, mass cool brands, and uncool brands within a subculture of urban streetwear enthusiasts

      • using exploratory factor analysis (studies 1-4)
      • confirmatory factor analysis - CFA (studies 5-8)
      • pretests were used to eliminate or replace items that either did not load highly onto the factor they were intended to measure or that cross-loaded onto multiple factors
      • studies 5-8 used to confirm the final model
      • the research used reflective instead of a formative model at each level for two reasons
        1. the logic underlying reflective models better fits the conceptualization of brand coolness
        1. the coefficients in formative models can vary with the number and structure of the measures and factors used

    • CORRELATES, CONSEQUENCES, AND MEDIATION
      • theoretical correlates: brand personality
        • brand personality is the set of human characteristics associated with a brand
        • serves a symbolic or self-expressive function for consumers, and it consists of five core dimensions
          • sophistication
          • competence
          • ruggedness
          • excitement
          • sincerity
        • brand personality perceptions should make that brand seem more, or less, cool, and they thus serve as antecedents of overall perceived brand coolness
        • multiple marketing and sociocultural elements (communications content, choice of endorsers) that shape these brand personality perceptions should also simultaneously shape the perceived coolness of the brand
        • difficult to empirically determine which perceptual changes came first
      • theoretical consequences
        • nomological model also estimated the effects of overall brand coolness on several different dependent variables
          • SBC
          • brand love
          • WTP
          • willingness to spread WOM
          • brand attitudes
          • brand familiarity
          • brand exposure
          • whether the brand commands a price premium
          • satisfaction
          • delight
          • pride
        • consumers view coolness as a desirable trait
        • brand coolness includes multiple characteristics that consumer consider desirable
          • hypothesis: brand coolness should predict consumers' overall attitude toward the brand, and include brand attitude valence as a consequence of brand coolness
          • brand coolness should also increase several other types of distinct positive feelings toward the brand
        • brand coolness also has components that are value-expressive in nature, including positive autonomy, rebellion, high status, subcultural appeal, and iconic symbolism
          • hypothesis: brand coolness will strengthen SBC because SBC increases as a brand's symbolic aspects become more consistent with a consumer's aspirational reference groups
        • both SBC and brand love tend to increase consumers' WTP for and likelihood of discussing (WOM) a brand

  • EXPERIMENT: MANIPULATING THE CHARACTERISTICS OF COOL BRANDS
    • this study have found that cool brands have different characteristics than uncool brands, but have not yet examined whether we can increase the extent to which a brand seems cool by experimentally manipulating the characteristics of brand coolness
    • in this study, the researcher manipulated the description of a watch brand to orthogonally vary the:
      • desirability (i.e., extraordinariness, aesthetic appeal, and excitement),
      • positive autonomy (i.e., originality and authenticity)
      • rebellion
      • popularity
    • constraints:
      • did not manipulate the extent to which the brand seemed iconic or subcultural
      • contrasted the cool with uncool brands rather than distinguishing between mass and niche cool brands
    • single-exposure experiment provides a conservative test of whether the characteristics influence perceptions of brand coolness
    • the study predicted that:
      • the brand would seem more cool when participants read that it was more (rather than less) desirable, autonomous, rebellious, popular, and high status
      • coolness would in turn influence participants' attitudes, WTP for, and likelihood of spreading WOM about the brand

    METHOD

    • participants (N: 368, 34% female, mean age 36, all located in the US) from MTurk (Amazon Mechanical Turk)
    • reading check at the beginning, filtered out 11 respondents
    • participants completed the study, titled "Online Review Survey", in which they were randomly assigned to a condition:
      • 2 (desirability : high, low) x
      • 2 (autonomy: high, low) x
      • 2 (rebellion: high, low) x
      • 2 (status: high, low) x
      • 2 (popularity: high, low) between-subjects experiment
    • participants subsequently completed a series of measures, including brand coolness, brand attitude, WTP, WOM, effectiveness of the manipulations using the full brand coolness scale from Studies 6-8, age, gender, and native language

    RESULTS

    • brand coolness
      • the analysis revealed main effects of:
        • desirability
        • autonomy
        • status
        • popularity
        • rebellion
      • participants perceived the brand to be more cool when it was described as:
        • being desirable (M = 4.26 vs. M = 3.27)
        • autonomous (M = 4.41 vs. M = 3.15)
        • high status (M = 4.01 vs. M = 3.54)
        • popular (M = 4.25 vs. M = 3.29)
        • rebellious (M = 3.98 vs. M = 3.56)
        • than when it lacked these qualities
      • none of the interactions were significant, which suggests that each characteristic additively influences perceived coolness
    • indirect effects on attitude, WTP, and WOM
      • one comprehensive SEM path model using LISREL (n = 368) were used, which allowed for all direct and indirect effects
      • brand coolness, in turn, significantly influenced the three dependent variables: brand attitude (.84), WTP (.54), and WOM (.82)
      • the effect of desirability, autonomy, popularity, status, and rebellion on the three dependent variables was at least partially mediated by brand coolness in each case
      • however, some significant direct effects of the manipulated brand characteristics on the dependent variables also emerged, though these direct effects are hard to interpret because of possible multicollinearity, remaining measurement error, or omitted mediators

    DISCUSSION

    • the experiment confirmed that increasing the extent to which a brand seems desirable, autonomous, rebellious, high status, and popular increases the extent to which it is perceived to be cool
    • brand coolness, in turn, influences several consequence vari- ables, including the extent to which consumers hold a favorable attitude toward the brand as well as their WTP for and willing- ness to discuss the brand with others
    • the experiment suggests that the effects of the characteristics of brand coolness on overall perceptions of coolness and on its downstream con- sequences (e.g., brand attitudes) are additive, though future research will need to further explore factors that moderate or interact with the different characteristics of brand coolness
  • GENERAL DISCUSSION

    THE LIFE CYCLE OF COOLNESS

    • this research additionally contributes to theory on the dynamic nature of coolness (e.g., Gladwell 1997) and brands (e.g., Parmentier and Fischer 2014) by exploring how the characteristics of cool brands change as a brand becomes niche cool, transitions from niche to mass cool, and eventually begins to lose its cool

    MANAGERIAL IMPLICATIONS

    • for many product categories and consumer segments, a brand‚Äôs perceived coolness is an important factor in driving its success, and managers have long sought to figure out how to give their brands this mysterious quality
    • the research resulting a scale provides a valuable tool for helping firms create and manage cool brands
    • the scale components can also be used for pretesting and evaluating different marketing and communication programs that are designed to increase or maintain a brand‚Äôs perceived coolness
    • how should managers respond if their brand is not scoring high enough on one or more component characteristics of brand coolness? they will need to reinforce the image of the band on the characteristic or characteristics it is lacking
    • firms will also need to assess whether their brand is currently niche cool, mass cool, or uncool to understand how to best manage the brand‚Äôs characteristics

    LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE RESEARCH OPPORTUNITIES

    many important questions remain for future research

    • how brand coolness relates to nomologically related constructs, especially brand personality
    • this study did not have access to multitrait-multimethod data, which are necessary for more definitive conclusions in this regard, as well as for a stronger estimate of common methods bias
    • this study did not formally investigate cross-cultural differences
    • examine the relationship between the specific coolness components, overall brand cool- ness, and downstream consequences such as brand attitudes, WOM, and WTP
